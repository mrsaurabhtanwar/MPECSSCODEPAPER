\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

The central contribution is the integration of two complementary
mechanisms: an adaptive regularization-parameter update driven by the
implemented homotopy residual, and a multiplier sign test derived from
the theoretical characterizations of Scheel and
Scholtes~\cite{ScheelScholtes2000}. Together with the Phase~III
certification pipeline, these components enable the systematic targeting
of B-stationary points in MPECs.

The theoretical analysis established that MPECSS inherits the
convergence guarantees of Scholtes regularization, ensuring
C-stationarity under standard assumptions. More importantly, when the
internal multiplier sign test passes, the algorithm can promote the
iterate to a B-stationary solution through the subsequent Phase~III
LICQ/LPEC certification logic. This represents a significant
advancement over standard regularization methods, which provide no
built-in mechanism for diagnosing or certifying stronger stationarity
concepts.

Comprehensive numerical experiments across three major benchmark collections demonstrated the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. On the MacMPEC collection (191 problems), MPECSS achieved a convergence rate of 94\%; on MPECLib (92 problems), 74\% overall convergence with 66\% certified as B-stationary; and on NOSBENCH (603 problems), 93\%. Among converged MPECLib problems, the B-stationarity certification rate reaches 90\%. These results highlight MPECSS as an open-source MPEC solver and benchmarking platform evaluated on all three benchmark suites in the present study. The implementation relies entirely on open-source software---Python, CasADi~\cite{AnderssonGillisHornRawlingsDiehl2019}, and IPOPT~\cite{WachterBiegler2006} with the MUMPS linear solver---eliminating dependencies on commercial tools such as MATLAB, SNOPT, Gurobi, or the HSL library.

First, extending the theoretical framework to weaker constraint qualifications such as MPEC-MFCQ would broaden the applicability of the convergence results. Second, incorporating second-order sufficient conditions could provide local convergence rate guarantees. Third, the adaptive parameter update strategy could potentially be enhanced through machine learning techniques that predict optimal reduction factors based on problem structure. Fourth, parallelization of the multistart feasibility phase and the restoration strategies could improve scalability for large-scale applications. Finally, extending MPECSS to handle Mathematical Programs with Vanishing Constraints (MPVCs) and other structured equilibrium problems represents a natural generalization.
